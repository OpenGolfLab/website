Every spring the same ad finds me: a robot, a wind tunnel, and a promise of "up to 14 more yards." And every spring, somewhere in the back of my head: *my driver is five years old. Am I the sucker holding a persimmon here?*

This year, instead of wondering, I tested it. Forty shots with my 2019 gamer, forty with a current head set to the same loft, same shaft flex, same ball, alternating in blocks, one garage morning. If you have a sim, you can run this exact experiment for the price of a demo club and an hour — and I'd argue it's the only driver review that has ever mattered for your game, because it's the only one hit with your swing.

## What actually changed since your driver was new

Worth knowing before you test, so you know what to even look for. The USGA capped face spring effect over two decades ago — a 2019 face and a 2026 face are pressed against the same legal limit. Nobody is selling you meaningfully more ball speed *on a pure strike*, whatever the robot says.

What the last several years of driver development actually bought is off-center behavior. Higher MOI heads twist less when you miss the middle, so a heel strike keeps more ball speed and takes on less wild spin. The marketing word is "forgiveness," which sounds like a participation trophy, but the measurable version is: **your bad drives get shorter less and curve less.** The center of the face is a solved problem. The rest of the face is where the R&D budget went.

Which means the upgrade question is really a question about you: how often do you miss the center, and by how much? A scratch player flushing it gains almost nothing. A twice-a-month player spraying it gains real, measurable yards — just not on the swing the ad filmed.

## What my test showed

My numbers, for what one golfer's morning is worth: the new head carried **3 yards farther on the median** — inside the noise of a 40-shot sample, honestly — but the spread told a different story. My lateral dispersion went from **±26 yards to ±21**. The longest drives barely moved. The bad ones moved a lot.

Three median yards would never justify the price of a new driver. Five fewer yards of miss, both directions, on every mishit? That's not a distance upgrade, that's a fairway-percentage upgrade, and [fairways are worth strokes](/blog/how-many-strokes-is-dispersion-worth). That's what I ended up paying for — knowingly, with my own numbers, which is a very different feeling than paying for a robot's.

Your result will be different. That's the entire point. You might find your gamer gives up nothing — in which case congratulations, you just saved $600 with one range session.

## How to run it yourself

The short version (the [full fitting protocol](/blog/run-your-own-club-fitting-in-your-sim) gets its own post):

- **Get a demo head**, matched as closely as you can on loft and shaft. Most big-box stores will lend one; some brands ship demos.
- **Same ball for everything**, and [the ball matters more than you'd think](/blog/does-the-ball-you-hit-into-your-screen-matter).
- **Alternate blocks of five**, gamer and demo, until you have 40 shots each. Alternating cancels out warm-up and fatigue; hitting all 40 with one club first does not.
- **Judge medians and spread, never bests.** Your longest drive with the new club is a lottery ticket. The median is your game.
- **Sleep on it and retest once.** New-club adrenaline is real and it's worth about half a club.

Golf Sim Analytics has an [A/B fitting dashboard](/golf-sim-analytics) built for exactly this — label the two clubs, hit, and it keeps the medians and dispersion honest while you're busy being excited.

## Where this is going

One golfer's A/B test is an anecdote with good intentions. What I actually want to know is what *thousands* of these comparisons say — does a 2020 head really give up 20% of its miss protection to a 2026 head at a 12-handicap's strike quality, or is that number marketing all the way down? That's a question only community data can answer, and it's [on the roadmap](/lab#roadmap): club model and year are coming to contributions, so the Lab can eventually publish old-vs-new deltas measured on real golfers instead of robots.

Until then: the [driver spectrum](/lab/gear) shows where every current head sits between forgiveness and distance, and the [street-price chart](/lab/gear#pricing) shows what patience is worth — last year's "innovation" is this year's discount. My advice, having done the test: buy width, not length. Length is mostly already in your swing. Width is what they've actually been building.
