I've paid for two professional fittings in my life. Both times I hit maybe a dozen swings per club, standing on a mat I'd never stood on, with a fitter watching, a range ball in play, and a low-grade awareness that the recommendation engine and the cash register were the same machine. Both times I walked out with a confident answer built on about as much data as a coin flip.

I'm not against fitters — a good one sees things in your delivery that no chart will. But the *data* half of a fitting, the "which of these two clubs performs better for you" half? Your garage does that better than any retail bay, because your garage has the one thing a fitting can't buy: unlimited swings, your ball, your mat, no audience, and no salesman's clock ticking.

Here's the protocol I've settled on after a year of doing this badly and then less badly.

## The protocol

**Get the candidates on equal footing.** Same loft (or as close as the adapters allow), same-ish shaft weight and flex. If you're testing shafts, use the same head. Change one variable, like your chemistry teacher said.

**Warm up off the books.** Fifteen, twenty swings, uncounted. Your first swings of a session are 2–3 mph slow and strike-poor, and if they land in one club's column, that club loses unfairly. In Golf Sim Analytics, just don't start the [A/B session](/golf-sim-analytics) until you're loose.

**Alternate blocks of five.** Five with club A, five with club B, repeat until you're at 40 shots each. This is the single most important rule. Fatigue, back tightness, and finding your rhythm all drift across a session — alternating spreads that drift evenly across both clubs instead of gifting it to whichever club you hit second.

**Same ball, every shot.** Not "same model," the same handful. And know [what your ball choice does to your numbers](/blog/does-the-ball-you-hit-into-your-screen-matter) before you test wedges especially.

**Stop at 40, not at tired.** Speed decays measurably after 60–70 full swings in my logs. Data from an exhausted swing is data about exhaustion. If you want 60 shots per club, take two sessions — which you should anyway, see below.

## How to read the result

Three numbers per club, and none of them is "longest":

- **Median carry.** The middle shot is your game. The best shot is a story you'll tell.
- **Spread (10th to 90th percentile of carry).** Two clubs with the same median but different spreads are not the same club — the tighter one is the one you can pick a landing number with.
- **Lateral width.** How wide is the pattern, and is it biased one direction? This is usually where a modern head vs. an old one [actually shows up](/blog/is-your-old-driver-costing-you-distance) — and it's worth more strokes than carry for [most golfers](/blog/how-many-strokes-is-dispersion-worth).

On sample size: medians are noisy below about 30 shots — with 20 shots per club, a 4-yard median gap is as likely to be sampling luck as signal. At 40 per club, differences of about 4+ yards of carry or 15%+ of width start meaning something. Below that, call it a tie and keep your money.

## The three ways you'll fool yourself anyway

**New-toy adrenaline.** You will swing harder at the shiny one. You won't feel yourself doing it. This is worth 1–2 mph and it fades in about a week — which is why anything that wins on day one has to *defend the title* in a second session a few days later before money moves. Half my "clear winners" haven't survived the rematch.

**One good block.** Ten shots in a row where the demo is perfect will feel like destiny. It's a hot streak, they happen constantly in 40-shot samples, and it's exactly what medians exist to ignore. Don't peek at running results mid-session; it changes how you swing. Hit, then read.

**Testing on your best day.** If you only A/B when you're swinging great, you're fitting the club to a golfer who rarely shows up. My rule now: the rematch session has to happen on a normal day, ideally a slightly tired one. I want the club that helps the Tuesday-night version of me.

## When the answer is "keep your money"

Most of my A/B tests have ended in ties, and I've come to think that's the protocol working, not failing. A tie means the $600 was going to buy paint. The whole reason to test at home with real sample sizes is that "no measurable difference" is a result a retail fitting is structurally incapable of delivering — nobody's rent gets paid by it.

The wins, when they come, are unambiguous and oddly calm: no launch-day excitement, just a wider gap than noise explains, twice in a row. That's what buying a club on evidence feels like. It's less fun than the ads. The golf is better though.
