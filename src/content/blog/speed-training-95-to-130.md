Two summers ago my driver swing was 94.8 miles per hour. I know the number to the decimal because it was the first thing my new launch monitor ever told me, and I remember being quietly offended by it.

This month my five best swings averaged 130.1, with a session max of 134.2. Same garage. Same guy, more or less.

I know exactly how that sounds, which is why this post exists. I logged every session for two years, and rather than tell you speed training "changed my game" — the sentence every training-aid ad ends with — I'd rather just show you the whole chart, including the three months where nothing happened and I nearly sold everything.

A note on the data: the charts below are rebuilt from my training log into monthly averages, and the [raw CSV is here](/data/speed-journey.csv) if you want to poke at it. "Avg best 5" means the average of my five fastest driver swings that month. Everything was measured — first on a Rapsodo into a net, later on a Mevo+ Gen 2 in the garage sim. None of it is from memory, because memory is a liar about this stuff.

## Where 95 came from

Some context, because starting points matter. I was not a golfer in any meaningful sense. I played once, maybe twice a year, shot mid-90s, and thought that was roughly the natural order of things. Two years ago I decided to actually get serious, and because I'm the kind of person who can't do a hobby without a spreadsheet, the first purchase wasn't lessons — it was a launch monitor and a net.

The 94.8 baseline is what an untrained, twice-a-year golfer swings when he's trying reasonably hard. For reference, that's about average for a male amateur. The PGA Tour hovers around 115. I mention this so the axis on that first chart means something: 95 is not slow. It's normal. That's the point of this whole post.

## The free speed nobody mentions

Here's the part that surprised me most, and it happened before any actual speed training: I gained almost five miles an hour by accident.

From July 2024 to January 2025 I did nothing but hit balls into a net and try to stop the contact from being embarrassing. Strike quality, tempo, a grip change I found on YouTube. Speed wasn't the goal — the Rapsodo was mostly there to tell me how bad the strike was. And yet: 94.8 to 99.4, no protocol, no weighted sticks, nothing.

Turns out a twice-a-year golfer's swing leaks speed everywhere — early extension, a cast, contact out on the toe. Cleaning up the strike doesn't just tighten dispersion; the same effort starts arriving at the ball as clubhead speed instead of as compensation. Nobody sells this, because there's nothing to sell. It's just reps with feedback.

If you take one thing from this post and you're at 95: your first five mph are probably already in your swing, waiting for you to stop wasting them.

## The garage changed the math

January 2025, I built the sim. Projector, impact screen, a Mevo+ Gen 2, and a winter's worth of marital goodwill. I've written elsewhere about [why capturing every shot matters](/blog/gspro-has-no-memory), but for speed specifically, the sim did something subtler: it made every single swing produce a number, automatically, without me squinting at a phone app between shots.

That matters more than it should. Speed training is boring. It's swinging as hard as you can, resting, and doing it again, three times a week, for months. The thing that kept me doing it wasn't discipline — it was that the number was always on the wall, and the [long-term progression chart](/golf-sim-analytics) was always quietly filling in. You don't skip sessions when skipping leaves a visible hole.

February 2025, I started actual overspeed work — lighter and heavier training clubs, swung at max intent, short sessions. The first block did exactly what the research says it does: 99.4 in January, 108.0 by June. Eight and a half miles an hour in five months. I felt like a genius. I was, briefly, insufferable.

## And then it stopped

July, August, September 2025: 108.6, 108.9, 109.4.

![Bar chart of month-over-month speed gains, showing strong early gains, a three-month stretch of almost nothing in mid-2025, then steady gains after adding strength work](/images/blog/speed-monthly-gains.jpg)

That's a mile and a half an hour in three months, most of it measurement noise. The protocol hadn't changed. The effort hadn't changed. My wrists ached, my swing thoughts multiplied, and the graph just... lay there. If you've done any speed training, you know this plateau, and if you're about to start, I want it on the record: the plateau is not a sign it stopped working. It's a sign the easy adaptations are spent.

I did almost quit here. What's the point of a training log if I'm honest about everything except that.

## The boring fix

What broke the plateau was embarrassingly unoriginal: I started lifting. Nothing exotic — a barbell, the basic lifts, twice a week in the same garage, plus more overall swing volume now that every swing was being counted anyway.

October 2025 through this July: 109.4 to 130.1. Twenty miles an hour in ten months, my two best months ever (+2.4 in November and February), and — this is the part I didn't expect — the gains didn't taper the way the first block's did. The overspeed work had apparently been writing checks my body couldn't cash. Strength raised the ceiling; the swings I'd already grooved cashed it out.

I want to be careful here, because this is where speed-training stories usually turn into a supplement ad. So, plainly: I don't know how much of that 20 mph was the lifting versus the accumulated volume versus something else. I didn't run a controlled experiment on myself. What I know is the order things happened in, and that the flat line bent exactly when the training stimulus changed. Take from that what you will.

## Did it make me better at golf, though

Fair question, since a launch monitor happily rewards things that don't cash on a course.

![Two-panel chart: clubhead speed rising from 95 to 130 mph on top, handicap index falling from 19.4 to 1.6 below](/images/blog/speed-vs-handicap.jpg)

My first posted handicap index, November 2024, was 19.4. It's 1.6 today. But look at the shape of the two lines, because they don't move together as neatly as you'd hope. The handicap kept falling through the speed plateau — that was short game hours, which no speed chart will ever show you. And the fastest speed gains, last winter, bought me almost nothing on the card for two months because I suddenly had 25 extra yards of carry and no idea where anything landed. Every gap in my bag had moved. My 7-iron became my old 5-iron, my misses got proportionally wider, and I had to re-learn my own yardages from scratch. (The [gapping dashboards](/golf-sim-analytics) earned their keep that month.)

Speed made the golf easier. It did not make the golf good. Those were separate projects that happened to share a garage.

## What a mile an hour actually buys

Across 150 logged sessions, my carry tracked speed at about 3.1 yards per mph:

![Scatter plot of 150 sessions, session speed vs median carry, with a fitted line of roughly 3.1 yards per mph](/images/blog/speed-to-carry.jpg)

Honest footnote on that number: it's fatter than pure ball-speed physics says it should be (~2.6 yd/mph), because my contact improved over the same two years — better strike rode along with more speed. Call it 2.5 to 3 yards per mph in the real world. Which means the *achievable* version of this story — not mine, the normal one — is still a very good deal.

## If you're starting from 95

You probably shouldn't chase 130. I got there on a stack of advantages that don't transfer: a garage twenty feet from my kitchen, a personality that finds hitting the same shot 400 times soothing, two years, and a body that held up (mostly — respect the wrist soreness, take the rest days, ramp in over weeks, and none of this is medical advice).

But the first eight to ten mph? Based on everything I logged, that's on the table for almost any golfer swinging in the 90s, and most of it is cheap:

- **Free speed first.** Fix contact with feedback. That was nearly +5 for me before training even started.
- **Overspeed second.** A basic protocol, three short sessions a week. That was the next +8.
- **Expect the wall.** Somewhere around month five or six the line goes flat. That's normal, it's not the end, and it's where most people quit.
- **Strength is the multiplier.** Boring, effective, also just good for you.
- **Measure everything.** Not because data swings the club, but because a visible streak is the only motivation that survives February.

Ten mph is 25 to 30 yards. That's a 7-iron into greens you used to play with a 5. You don't need my number; you need your own, plus ten.
